## [Selling Mood Point: The Trap of Marketing PPMs]

The modern beauty consumer is being systematically misled by the manipulation of INCI (International Nomenclature of Cosmetic Ingredients) labeling. We frequently see products claiming "90% Salmon Extract" in large, bold font on their packaging. The consumer assumes they are receiving a potent dose of PDRN.

The industry reality is far more cynical.

Brands are substituting "Salmon Egg Extract"—essentially a cheap, water-based protein broth—for actual bioactive Sodium DNA. Because the extract is mostly water, they can claim a high percentage without violating labeling laws. Meanwhile, the genuine active ingredient, Sodium DNA (PDRN), is buried at the very bottom of the ingredient list, often alongside phenoxyethanol or other preservatives, at concentrations likely below 0.1%.

This is the "marketing PPM" trap. Real PDRN is an expensive, isolated pharmaceutical-grade polymer. When you see a bottle for $15, you are not buying regeneration; you are buying water, humectants, and a label that banks on your lack of regulatory knowledge.

## [HCOS Lab Manual: The 3-Step Screening Protocol for Amazon Products]

If you are looking to audit the quality of an Amazon PDRN serum like an R&D professional, apply this technical screening protocol:

1. **Verify the INCI Position:** Search for "Sodium DNA" or "PDRN" in the ingredient list. If it is listed after the preservative system or the fragrance/botanical extracts, the formula is functionally inert.
2. **Audit for Stabilization Claims:** Look for technical keywords like "Encapsulation," "Liposome," "Delivery System," or "Stabilized Polymer." If a brand cannot describe how they prevent oxidation, they haven't solved it.
3. **Evaluate Packaging Architecture:** PDRN has no business being in a dropper bottle or transparent plastic. Exposure to air and UV light destroys it. Look for airless pumps and UV-blocking (opaque) containers. If the packaging is transparent, the product was compromised at the point of sale.
